  <h1 class="pageTitle">MESLO — Materials</h1>
  <p class="lead">A structured approach to material cost clarity, pricing discipline, and margin protection.</p>

  <p>Materials are often the largest direct cost in a project—and one of the most common sources of hidden margin loss. MESLO treats materials as a controlled input, not a rough assumption.</p>

  <h2>What “Materials” Covers</h2>
  <ul>
    <li>Raw materials and purchased goods</li>
    <li>Supplier pricing and quotes</li>
    <li>Freight, delivery, and handling</li>
    <li>Waste, overage, and shrinkage</li>
    <li>Lead times and availability constraints</li>
    <li>Price volatility and escalation risk</li>
  </ul>

  <h2>Why It Matters</h2>
  <p>Unstructured material inputs lead to:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Underpriced bids</li>
    <li>Margin erosion during execution</li>
    <li>Rework due to shortages or incorrect quantities</li>
    <li>Cash flow pressure from poor purchasing assumptions</li>
  </ul>
  <p>A disciplined materials system ensures:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Accurate cost visibility before a bid is submitted</li>
    <li>Consistent pricing across projects</li>
    <li>Alignment between estimating, purchasing, and field use</li>
  </ul>

  <h2>Common Failure Points</h2>
  <ul>
    <li>Using outdated or informal supplier pricing</li>
    <li>Ignoring waste factors or applying them inconsistently</li>
    <li>Missing freight, tax, or delivery costs</li>
    <li>No linkage between takeoff quantities and actual procurement</li>
    <li>Treating materials as a lump sum instead of line-item inputs</li>
  </ul>

  <h2>What a Structured System Looks Like</h2>
  <p>A MESLO-aligned materials system includes:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Defined quantities tied to takeoffs and scope</li>
    <li>Current pricing sourced and documented</li>
    <li>Waste factors applied consistently by material type</li>
    <li>Delivery and logistics costs captured upfront</li>
    <li>Clear assumptions documented for review and revision</li>
  </ul>
  <p>This creates a repeatable model where:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>inputs are visible</li>
    <li>assumptions are testable</li>
    <li>outcomes are predictable</li>
  </ul>

  <h2>For CPAs and Advisors</h2>
  <p>Materials are a primary driver of cost variance.</p>
  <p>A structured approach allows you to:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Validate whether estimates reflect real input costs</li>
    <li>Identify where margin loss originates (pricing vs. execution)</li>
    <li>Assess consistency across jobs and time periods</li>
    <li>Support more reliable forecasting and job-level reporting</li>
  </ul>

  <h2>How It Connects to the Rest of MESLO</h2>
  <p>Materials do not stand alone.</p>
  <p>They directly impact:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Labor (production assumptions tied to quantities)</li>
    <li>Equipment (handling, installation, and logistics)</li>
    <li>Subcontractors (scope clarity and bid normalization)</li>
    <li>Overhead and profit (accuracy of total job cost)</li>
  </ul>
  <p>When materials are unclear, the entire estimate becomes unstable.</p>

  <h2>Outcome</h2>
  <p>When materials are structured correctly:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Estimates are faster and more consistent</li>
    <li>Cost visibility improves before work begins</li>
    <li>Field execution aligns with pricing assumptions</li>
    <li>Margins stabilize across projects</li>
  </ul>
